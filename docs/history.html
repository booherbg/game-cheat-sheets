A Short History of Trick-Taking (and Why Euchre Won the Midwest)

Trick-taking is one of the great ideas in card games: you lead, everyone else follows if they can, and the best card (or the right trump) takes the pile. No one knows who invented it. We do know it was in Europe by the early 1400s - games like Karnöffel in Germany (attested by the 1420s) used uneven card powers before anyone had settled on a single trump suit.[1] Then, around 1440 in Italy, someone added a permanent fifth suit of picture cards that always beat the other four. We call those cards tarots. The tarot deck was a trick-taking pack first; the fortune-telling came later.[2] From there the idea spread: a fixed trump, follow suit, win the trick. It stuck.

By the 1700s, the English had turned the idea into Whist - four players, two teams, no bidding, just play. Quiet, serious, and so popular that Edmond Hoyle wrote A Short Treatise on the Game of Whist in 1742 and the book stayed in print for a century.[3][4] Whist was the game of gentlemen and coffeehouses; it had rules, literature, and a reputation. It also had a flaw, at least in the eyes of players who wanted more say in the action: the trump suit was decided by a single turned card. No choice. So in the 1890s a variant caught fire - Biritch (Russian Whist), where the dealer could name trump or no trump, and one hand was laid face-up as the "dummy." That game became Bridge; contract bridge (1925) replaced Whist almost everywhere in polite play within a few decades.[5]

Meanwhile, the origins of another card game were developing as the story of the German-American immigrant played out.

Jucker becomes Euchre

In the Alsace and the Palatinate - the strip of land that has spent centuries between France and Germany - people were playing a game called Juckerspiel. "Jucker" was the name; the game gave the Jack a starring role. The Jack (in German, Bauer - farmer) outranked the Ace in the trump suit. There was a word for taking all five tricks: Marsch. The earliest clear mention we have is 1792, in a German dictionary, calling it a Palatinate game. By the early 1800s it was in the Rhineland, in stories, in the air.[6][7] And when German-speaking immigrants left for America, the game came with them. Card game historian David Parlett argues that "on linguistic grounds alone there can be no doubt as to Euchre's origin in the Alsatian game of Juckerspiel as brought to America by German immigrants."[6]

However, in the New World it didn't stay as "Juckerspiel". English-speaking players heard "Jucker" and wrote it down as Euchre (early spellings included "Eucre" and "Uker").[6][8] The Jack stayed on top in trump and kept a trace of the old name: we still call it the right bower, and the other Jack of the same color the left bower - "bower" from Bauer. The word for a clean sweep stayed too: we still say march (from Marsch).[6][8] The game didn’t need a rulebook to cross the ocean. It had people.

By 1810, "Eucre" was already turning up in American gaming houses next to Whist. By 1829 it was on Midwest steamboats - the riverboats that moved goods and people up and down the Ohio and the Mississippi.[8] Deckhands, travelers, and settlers had hours to kill. Euchre was fast, portable, and easy to teach. It spread up the rivers and into the new states. In 1844 the first written American rules appeared.[8] A few years later, Euchre packs began to include an extra card that could stand in for any trump when you needed it. That card was the Joker - and Euchre is the reason it exists in the standard deck at all; jokers first appeared in Euchre packs in the 1850s.[8]

For a while, Euchre was often called the national card game of the United States. It wasn’t the only one, but it was the one that had caught the country’s attention.[8] Then the country changed. Bridge took over in clubs and parlors. Poker and Rummy took over in others. Euchre didn’t die. It dug in where it had already put down roots: the Midwest.[9]

A note on dialect - 'Bower' vs 'Bar'

One more trace of the journey shows up in how the word is said, even as of this writing today in 2026. In most of the Euchre Belt, "bower" is pronounced like "flower" (two syllables). But in Ohio - especially western Ohio - many players say "bar" instead: right bar, left bar.[13][14] Rulebooks and websites usually spell it "bower"; Ohio-focused sources list "Bower (AKA Bar)" and note that "bar" is a common dialect pronunciation there.[13] The likely reason is immigration and oral tradition, not print. Bauer in German is two syllables; in Palatine or local dialects it may have been shortened or flattened in speech. When the game was passed on by word of mouth in German-heavy Ohio, the word may have been heard and repeated as a single syllable - "bar" - and that pronunciation stuck in the same communities where the game itself had put down roots. So you get two reflexes of the same German word: "bower" in the books, "bar" at the table where the rules were passed on via oral tradition in a local dialect.[15]

The Euchre Belt

If you draw a band across the northern United States - Pennsylvania, Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Kentucky, Michigan - you get what players and writers sometimes call the Euchre Belt. The game is still the default there. Family reunions, bars, dorm rooms, and kitchen tables: someone’s always dealing. Michigan gets called the buckle of that belt.[9][10] Ask why and you get the same answer again and again: Because we grew up with it. Parents and grandparents taught it. It’s the game you play when the aunts and uncles are in town. That’s not a small thing. Culture doesn’t need a reason to persist beyond "we’ve always done it." Euchre had a head start in those states. The people who’d brought it had settled there. The game became part of the local grammar - something you assume everyone knows. The exact reason for euchre’s lasting popularity in Michigan is still debated, but family tradition and intergenerational teaching are the usual explanation.[10]

Ohio, Indiana, and Michigan all drew heavy German settlement in the 19th century. So did parts of Illinois and Pennsylvania. The map of "where Germans went" and "where Euchre is still the game" overlap. It’s not a perfect overlap - nothing in cultural history is - but it’s there.[9] Immigration didn’t just bring new workers or new last names. It brought games. Games are lightweight. You don’t need much to play. You need a deck, a table, and someone who remembers the rules. So the games that arrived with the first generations stuck where those generations put down roots. In the Midwest, one of those games was Euchre. In other pockets you got Pinochle (also German in origin - from Bezique and regional games like Binokel, carried over by immigrants in the late 1800s; the name comes from Binokel/Binocle).[11] Pinochle added meld: you score for marriages, runs, and "around" combos before a single trick is played. Different flavor, same family. Same story of a game crossing the ocean in someone’s memory.

Hearts and Spades belong to the same big family - trick-taking, follow suit, win or avoid tricks - but they have different lineages. Hearts came from European penalty games (Reversis and the like), then took shape in America in the 1880s; the Standard Hoyle of 1887 said it had been played in America for about five years. The Queen of Spades as the big penalty ("Black Lady") and "shooting the moon" came in later (e.g. 1909 for Black Lady).[12] Spades is an American game too, with fixed trump and bidding. Bridge is Whist’s direct heir: same structure, plus bidding and the dummy.[5] So you get a family tree: Tarot and the old European games at the root; Whist and Juckerspiel as two big branches; Bridge from Whist; Euchre and then the Joker from Jucker; Pinochle from Bezique and German meld games; Hearts and Spades as American inventions built on the same trick-taking skeleton.

Why it matters

Trick-taking is old. The idea has outlasted empires. What’s newer is how it got local. The same basic idea - lead, follow, trump, take the trick - shows up in dozens of games. Which one you play often depends on where you grew up and who taught you. In the Euchre Belt, that game is Euchre. The bowers and the march and the Joker are the dialect. In other places it’s Pinochle, or Spades, or Bridge, or something else. The games traveled with people. They still do. So the history of trick-taking isn’t just a list of rules. It’s a map of who went where, what they brought, and what they kept playing when they got there.
