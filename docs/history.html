A Short History of Trick-Taking (and Why Euchre Won the Midwest)

Trick-taking is one of the great ideas in card games: you lead, everyone else follows if they can, and the best card (or the right trump) takes the pile. No one knows who invented it. We do know it was in Europe by the early 1400s - games like Karnöffel in Germany (attested by the 1420s) used uneven card powers before anyone had settled on a single trump suit.[1] Then, around 1440 in Italy, someone added a permanent fifth suit of picture cards that always beat the other four. We call those cards tarots. The tarot deck was a trick-taking pack first; the fortune-telling came later.[2] From there the idea spread: a fixed trump, follow suit, win the trick. It stuck.

By the 1700s, the English had turned the idea into Whist - four players, two teams, no bidding, just play. Quiet, serious, and so popular that Edmond Hoyle wrote A Short Treatise on the Game of Whist in 1742 and the book stayed in print for a century.[3][4] Whist was the game of gentlemen and coffeehouses; it had rules, literature, and a reputation. It also had a flaw, at least in the eyes of players who wanted more say in the action: the trump suit was decided by a single turned card. No choice. So in the 1890s a variant caught fire - Biritch (Russian Whist), where the dealer could name trump or no trump, and one hand was laid face-up as the "dummy." That game became Bridge; contract bridge (1925) replaced Whist almost everywhere in polite play within a few decades.[5] Another branch of the same family - meld games like Bezique, carried by German immigrants in the late 1800s - would give America Pinochle, which took off in the 20th century and still has a strong following today.[11]

Meanwhile, an origin story born out of 19th century US expansion was unfolding. As German-American immigrants moved west they brought with them the foundations for a new card game that would embed itself permanently in the collective cultural consciousness of the early American Midwest. This game, of course, would become what we now know as Euchre.

How Alsacian Juckerspiel became American Euchre

In the Alsace and the Palatinate - the strip of land that has spent centuries between France and Germany - people played a game called Juckerspiel (or "Jucker" for short - named after the "Joker" card). In "Jucker", the Jack (or "Bauer") outranked the Ace in the trump suit, and the "Joker" was always the highest ranked trump card. The earliest clear mention we have is 1792, in a German dictionary, calling it a Palatinate game. By the early 1800s the game was in the Rhineland and showing up in larger Germanic popular culture.[6][7] Thus when German-speaking immigrants left for America, the game came with them. Card game historian David Parlett argues that "on linguistic grounds alone there can be no doubt as to Euchre's origin in the Alsatian game of Juckerspiel as brought to America by German immigrants."[6]

However, in the New World it didn't stay as "Juckerspiel". English-speaking players heard "Jucker" and wrote it down as Euchre (early spellings included "Eucre" and "Uker").[6][8] The Jack stayed on top in trump and kept traces of the old name: we still call it the right bower, and the other Jack of the same color the left bower; The word "bower" is derived from Bauer (German for "farmer"). The word for a clean sweep stayed too: we still say march (from Marsch).[6][8]. The final vestigial etymologic quirk remains in the root of the name of the game itself. "Euchre" of course derives from the "Jucker" in "Juckerspiel", which refers to the Joker card that could stand in for the highest trump. While the modern game has since dropped the Joker as part of standard card play, Euchre is actually the reason it exists in the standard deck at all; the joker card first appeared in Euchre packs in the 1850s.[8].

By 1810, "Eucre" was already turning up in American gaming houses next to Whist. By 1829 it was on Midwest steamboats - the riverboats that moved goods and people up and down the Ohio and the Mississippi.[8] Deckhands, travelers, and settlers had hours to kill. Euchre was fast, portable, and easy to teach. It spread up the rivers and into the new states. In 1844 the first written American rules appeared in The Whist Player's Hand-book by Thomas Mathews.[31][8]

In the 1844 Mathews text, Euchre appears alongside a game called Boston as an addendum to the primary Whist material.[31]. Notable differences from modern Euchre include using 32 cards (7-A) and playing 2, 3, 4 or 5 persons with 2 and 4 being most common; although it is not clear how partners are chosen when playing with 3 or 5 players. The rules state that the game plays to 5 using "Duce and Tray" to score points. The rules also focus on etiquette, including an interesting suggestion that "ordering up" should only be done when you have but one point to make - being that it is your adversaries deal and you are now stealing the deal (how rude!). The term Assist is mentioned as when the dealer's partner ordering up trump - because they are assisting the dealer in getting 3 points. Here we also see the origins of the Canadian Loner rule as a house rule to be set prior to the start of the game - such that if a partner assists the dealer, the dealer may go alone.

Through the late 19th century Euchre was known as the national card game of the United States. It wasn’t the only popular game, but it was the game that had caught the country’s attention.[8]. As American game culture invariablely adapted and consolidated, bridge, pinochle, poker and rummy rose in popularity in parlors and clubs. Euchre, however, dug in where it had already put down roots: the eastern Midwest.[9]

The Euchre Belt

If you draw a band across the northern United States - Pennsylvania, Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Kentucky, Michigan - you get what players and writers sometimes call the Euchre Belt. The game is still the default there. Family reunions, bars, dorm rooms, and kitchen tables: someone’s always dealing. Michigan gets called the buckle of that belt.[9][10] Ask why and you get the same answer again and again: Because we grew up with it. Parents and grandparents taught it. It’s the game you play when the aunts and uncles are in town. That’s not a small thing. Culture doesn’t need a reason to persist beyond "we’ve always done it." Euchre had a head start in those states. The people who’d brought it had settled there. The game became part of the local cultural context - something you assume everyone knows. The exact reason for euchre’s lasting popularity in Michigan is still debated, but family tradition and intergenerational teaching are the usual explanation.[10]

Ohio, Indiana, and Michigan all drew heavy German settlement in the 19th century. So did parts of Illinois and Pennsylvania. The map of "where Germans went" and "where Euchre is still the game" overlap. It’s not a perfect overlap - nothing in cultural history is - but it’s there.[9] Immigration didn’t just bring new workers or new last names. It brought games. Games are lightweight. You don’t need much to play. You need a deck, a table, and someone who remembers the rules. So the games that arrived with the first generations stuck where those generations put down roots. In the Midwest, one of those games was Euchre. In other pockets you got Pinochle (also German in origin - from Bezique and regional games like Binokel, carried over by immigrants in the late 1800s; the name comes from Binokel/Binocle).[11] Pinochle added meld: you score for marriages, runs, and "around" combos before a single trick is played. Different flavor, same family. Same story of a game crossing the ocean in someone’s memory.

A note on dialect - "Bower" vs "Bar"

One more trace of the journey shows up in how the word is pronounced, even as of this writing today in 2026. In most of the Euchre Belt, "bower" is pronounced like "flower" (two syllables). But in sections of Ohio - specifically western Ohio - many players say "bar" instead: right bar, left bar.[13][14] Rulebooks and websites usually spell it "bower"; Ohio-focused sources list "Bower (AKA Bar)" and note that "bar" is a common dialect pronunciation there.[13] The likely reason is immigration and oral tradition, but the true source isn't known. Bauer in German is two syllables. When the game was passed on by word of mouth in German-heavy Ohio, the word may have been heard and repeated as a single syllable - "bar" - and that pronunciation stuck in the same communities where the game itself had put down roots. So you get two dialects of the same German word: "bower" in the books, "bar" in the areas where the rules were passed on via oral tradition in a local dialect.[15]

On a personal note, I was raised in the Western Ohio culture that pronounces "bower" as "bar". I didn't hear the "bower" pronunciation until I played Euchre in college at the University of Cincinnati. My dad's side are heavy card players where Euchre is the default (along with Shanghai Rummy with 5+ players or Pinocle with my grandparents). My mom's side also played cards but her family is from North/Central Ohio. My maternal uncle remembers playing Euchre while attending High School in Western Ohio and being told that the teased about his pronunciation of "bower" instead of "bar".

Notes Cribbage, Whist, Hearts, Spades, and Pinochle

As an aside: Not all German-heritage settlement fed the Euchre Belt. Germans from Russia (Volga and Black Sea Germans) left Russia when their privileges were revoked (1870s onward) and settled in the Northern Plains—North Dakota, South Dakota, Minnesota, and nearby - a different migration path and era than the Ohio/Mississippi corridor.[16][17] In that region, the default card games wasn't Euchre. Minnesota Whist (a variant) was brought to the Upper Midwest by Norwegian immigrants;[18] Cribbage, of English origin, also took hold there.[19] In this way, Palatine and Alsatian river-corridor settlers birthed Euchre; Germans from Russia and Scandinavian neighbors in the Plains built a card culture around Whist and Cribbage instead.

Why Cribbage runs deep in the Upper Midwest. Cribbage was invented in England (Sir John Suckling, 1630s) and crossed the Atlantic with English settlers; it was especially popular in New England and was quickly taken up by sailors and fishermen - a two-player game that needed only a deck and a board.[19][20] In Canada and the Great Lakes, cribbage was a game of explorers, whalers, and the fur-trade era; the Hudson's Bay orbit and trade with Indigenous peoples left a trail of cribbage boards and play.[21] Railway and work camps did the rest. Cribbage boards from the 1880s survive from Canadian Pacific Railway construction crews in what is now Northwestern Ontario;[22] the same kind of portable, two-player pastime suited lumber camps and winter bunkhouses in Minnesota and along the border.

Wisconsin had cribbage in print in Milwaukee by 1841, and today Wisconsin, Michigan, and Minnesota still show the strongest concentration of cribbage play in the Midwest.[23] Minnesota's immigration included Irish who had worked as lumberjacks in Canada before moving south - one vector for British Isles and Canadian card-game culture into the Upper Midwest.[24] So cribbage's depth in Minnesota ties to its English origin, spread by sailors and work camps, and the same Anglo-Canadian and Irish migration streams that helped populate the region.

Hearts and Spades are both suit following trick taking games - but they have different lineages.

Hearts came from European penalty games (Reversis and the like), then took shape in America in the 1880s; the Standard Hoyle of 1887 said it had been played in America for about five years. The Queen of Spades as the big penalty ("Black Lady") and "shooting the moon" came in later (e.g. 1909 for Black Lady).[12]

Spades is a 20th-century American addition to that family. It belongs to the same Whist-derived line: partnership play, bidding, and a trump suit - in this case spades always trump. Bridge author George Coffin placed its introduction in Cincinnati, Ohio, between 1937 and 1939; from there it spread through the Midwest and then exploded during World War II on military bases - fast, interruptible, ideal for barracks and deployment - and after the war on college campuses. It is now one of the most played partnership card games in the United States.[25] The immigration and migration story is built in.

Bid Whist, a close relative (also Whist plus bidding), has deep roots in African America* culture. Black Enterprise (1999), citing research by Angel Beck (author of How to Play Bid Whist and the only syndicated bid whist columnist at the time), reports that the game has been a tradition since slavery (pre–Civil War): enslaved people developed their own adaptation of the card games they saw white people playing, primarily bridge and whist.[29] That origin is also documented in oral tradition and in Rise and Fly (Morrison & Lamb, 2005) and NPR’s coverage; white society sometimes dismissed it as "Black bridge." Pullman porters carried Bid Whist along the rails and into cities during the early 20th century; Black G.I.s played Spades and Bid Whist in the military and brought both games home.[26][27][28] So the same trick-taking idea - lead, follow, trump - shows up again in Spades and Bid Whist, this time tied to 20th-century America: the military, the Great Migration, and Black American community and kinship.

On the topic of Pinochle the game never became one region's default game. It spread instead as a layer on top of existing card culture: popular in German-American, Jewish, and Irish immigrant communities, and among people who already liked trick-taking or meld games.[11] So you get pockets and clubs nationwide rather than a single geography where "everyone" plays it. That visibility was enough to draw fire. During World War I, anti-German sentiment in the US was fierce; cities renamed sauerkraut "liberty cabbage" and German Shepherds "Alsatians." In Syracuse, New York, the city went so far as to outlawed pinochle as a German game.[30]

So, all of this to say that the history of modern trick-taking games is a fun exercise in culture, invention, and tradition. Tarot and the old European games sit at the base; Whist and Juckerspiel are two main lineages. From Whist come Bridge (bidding and the dummy) and, in 20th-century America, Spades; from the same Whist–Bridge line, Bid Whist grew out of Black American tradition since slavery (pre–Civil War). From Juckerspiel come Euchre and the Joker. Hearts is an American offshoot of European penalty games (avoid the wrong tricks), and Pinochle belongs to a different branch—Bezique and German meld games—where you score for melds first and tricks second. Cribbage sits on a different line altogether—no tricks, just counting and pegging—but it’s part of the same story: an English game that crossed the Atlantic with sailors and settlers and took root in the same regions and work camps (the Upper Midwest, the rails) where these other games were played.

Trick-taking games are old. The idea has outlasted empires. What’s newer are the local variants adapted to play as humans migrated and their culture followed them. The same basic idea - lead, follow, trump, take the trick - shows up in dozens of games. Which one you play often depends on where you grew up and who taught you. In the Euchre Belt, that game is Euchre. In other places it may be Whist, Spades, Bridge, Cribbage, Pinochle, Bid Whist, or something else entirely. Card games travel in the minds of the people who remember them. In many ways the history of trick-taking games is a map of who went where, what they brought with them, and what kept them playing when they got there.
